\section{Exploratory Data Analysis \& Strategic Prescriptions}

The following analysis draws on a ten-year transaction record (July 2012–December 2022)
comprising 646,945 orders, 714,669 line items, 91.5 million web sessions across six
acquisition channels, and complete inventory snapshots for four product categories.
Three independent data stories are developed; each traverses all four analytical levels—
\textit{Descriptive}, \textit{Diagnostic}, \textit{Predictive}, and \textit{Prescriptive}—to
derive actionable, quantified strategic recommendations.

% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Story 1: Deteriorating Conversion Efficiency Across All Channels
While Return Risk Remains Structurally Flat}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Descriptive.}
Cross-joining monthly order counts with monthly web-traffic sessions reveals six-channel
conversion efficiency (orders per 1,000 sessions) ranging from
\textbf{social\_media} (10.40) and \textbf{direct} (9.02) at the high end
to \textbf{organic\_search} (7.39) at the low end—a 41\% spread (Table~\ref{tab:channel}).
Average order value (AOV) is quasi-uniform at \$24,162–\$24,332, indicating that
price composition, not channel mix, drives revenue concentration.
\textbf{organic\_search} nevertheless generates the largest absolute net-sales volume
(\$4.39B, 28.9\% of total), owing to its dominant order share (181,495 orders, 28.1\%).
In stark contrast, return rates across all six channels span only 3.33\%–3.46\%,
a 13\,bp range that is economically negligible.

\textbf{Diagnostic.}
The near-zero dispersion in return rate eliminates post-purchase quality differences as a
meaningful channel differentiator.
The root cause of revenue concentration in a low-efficiency channel
(\textbf{organic\_search}) is a legacy budget and SEO investment structure that maximises
reach rather than conversion yield.
Critically, time-series OLS applied to monthly conversion efficiency reveals
\textit{uniformly negative trends} across every channel
(slopes: organic\_search $-0.093$, paid\_search $-0.095$, social\_media $-0.107$,
email\_campaign $-0.120$, direct $-0.121$, referral $-0.123$ orders/1,000 sessions per month),
indicating systemic audience saturation and rising customer-acquisition cost (CAC) across
the entire media portfolio.

\textbf{Predictive.}
Extrapolating current decay rates, \textbf{social\_media}—the highest-efficiency
channel—will converge to the current \textbf{organic\_search} baseline within
approximately 26 months.
If media mix is held constant, blended conversion efficiency will fall below 7.0 within
18 months, implying a CAC escalation of $\sim$15\% and a corresponding gross-profit-per-order
deterioration of \$30–\$50 at current AOV.

\textbf{Prescriptive.}
Because return risk is channel-agnostic, the dominant lever is conversion-yield
reallocation. \textit{Shifting 100,000 sessions from organic\_search to social\_media
generates +301 incremental orders, +\$7.29M net revenue, and +\$707K gross profit.}
We recommend a weekly budget-rebalancing rule: increase channel spend only when the
trailing 4-week \textit{orders\_per\_1k\_sessions} exceeds the cross-channel median
\textit{and} the channel's return rate does not exceed the 75th percentile.
A channel-level gross-margin guardrail (minimum \$2,100 GP per order) should gate
any new budget increment to prevent volume-driven margin dilution.

\subsection{Story 2: Promotions Beyond the 10\% Discount Threshold
Are Systematically Margin-Destructive}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Descriptive.}
Segmenting 714,669 line items by discount intensity yields four buckets whose gross-margin
profiles diverge sharply at the 10\% threshold:
$\leq$5\% discount (+17.24\%), 5–10\% (+0.87\%), 10–20\% ($-$10.85\%), and
$>$20\% ($-$24.56\%).
The promo-type lens reinforces this: non-promoted lines carry $+$19.96\% margin, while
\textit{percentage} promotions erode margin to $-$10.25\% and \textit{fixed}-amount
promotions destroy $-$63.28\%.
Across the full catalogue, 38.66\% of order lines carry at least one promotion, implying
that a substantial share of revenue is generated at a loss.

\textbf{Diagnostic.}
The margin inversion above 10\% discount is a structural artefact of COGS composition:
the average product margin at list price is $\sim$20\%, so any discount beyond 10 percentage
points fully extinguishes gross profit before accounting for fulfilment costs.
Fixed-amount promotions are disproportionately harmful because they scale absolute
discount independently of unit price, causing the discount-to-revenue ratio to balloon
on low-ticket SKUs.
The near-breakeven profile of the 5–10\% bucket (+0.87\%) indicates that this band
absorbs necessary price sensitivity without materially impairing the P\&L, making it
the natural guardrail ceiling.

\textbf{Predictive.}
If the current promo-mix (38.66\% promotional attachment) is maintained through seasonal
peak periods, blended gross-margin on promoted revenue will remain approximately
$-$14.5\%.
Deep-discount campaigns (fixed-amount or $>$20\% intensity) concentrated in Q4 will
amplify margin drag and could reduce consolidated gross profit by an estimated
\$150–\$200M over a two-year horizon under flat revenue assumptions.

\textbf{Prescriptive.}
Implement a \textit{discount guardrail framework}: (i)~cap the default promotional
ceiling at 10\%; (ii)~permit exceedance only for SKUs whose \textit{days\_of\_supply}
places them in the top overstock quartile, limiting deep discounts to genuine
inventory-clearance use cases; (iii)~deprecate fixed-amount promotion types entirely
and replace with percentage-based mechanics subject to the 10\% cap.
\textit{Simulation: redirecting 20\% of revenue currently in the $>$10\% discount
cohort (\$749.6M) into the 5–10\% bucket would improve gross profit by
\$96.1M}—a 4.4\% uplift on total gross profit.

\subsection{Story 3: Concurrent Overstock and Return Risk Are Locking
\$387.6M of Working Capital Across All Categories}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Descriptive.}
The most recent inventory snapshot reveals total on-hand inventory cost of \$387.6M
distributed across four categories.
\textbf{Streetwear} dominates with \$276.4M (71.3\% of total), an overstock rate of 82.65\%,
and an average days-of-supply (DOS) of 2,032 days—more than five years of forward cover.
\textbf{Outdoor} carries \$88.2M at 78.47\% overstock, while \textbf{Casual} (\$16.0M)
and \textbf{GenZ} (\$7.1M) exhibit the highest overstock rates in relative terms
(72.50\% and 84.09\%, respectively).
Mapping return-induced refund leakage onto gross profit reveals that \textbf{Casual}
has the highest refund-to-GP ratio (41.57\%), followed by \textbf{Streetwear} (34.89\%)
and \textbf{Outdoor} (29.48\%).

\textbf{Diagnostic.}
The coexistence of extreme overstock and non-trivial return rates creates a
\textit{double working-capital trap}: excess inventory immobilises capital that
cannot be recycled into higher-turn SKUs, while return refunds (\$406.7M for
Streetwear alone) directly erode the gross profit that would otherwise fund
markdown-driven clearance.
The root causes are misaligned demand forecasting—evidenced by DOS exceeding
2,000 days for Streetwear—and insufficient fit-guidance infrastructure (Q3 confirmed
\textit{wrong\_size} as the dominant Streetwear return reason), which generates
avoidable refund outflows.

\textbf{Predictive.}
If overstock rates continue at the current trajectory without active liquidation,
Streetwear's locked capital (\$276.4M) will continue to compound carrying costs
estimated at 15–20\% annually (storage, insurance, opportunity cost).
Simultaneously, size-driven return rates—structurally linked to the absence of
predictive sizing recommendations—will sustain Streetwear's refund-to-GP ratio
above 30\%, creating a self-reinforcing profitability leakage loop as each return
further delays inventory turnover.

\textbf{Prescriptive.}
Three coordinated interventions are recommended.
\textit{First}, execute a margin-guarded Streetwear liquidation programme:
target a 5\,pp reduction in overstock rate, which would free \$13.8M in working
capital; apply markdown pricing subject to the 10\% guardrail established in
Story~2 to avoid compounding the margin destruction documented there.
\textit{Second}, deploy a ML-powered fit-recommendation engine at checkout for
Streetwear and Casual: reducing Streetwear's refund amount by 10–15\% would
save \$40.7M–\$61.0M annually.
\textit{Third}, institute a category-level control tower with weekly KPI alerts
on five metrics: \textit{orders\_per\_1k\_sessions}, \textit{gross\_margin\_rate},
\textit{return\_rate}, \textit{overstock\_rate}, and \textit{refund\_to\_gp},
enabling pre-emptive course correction before leakage compounds.

\subsection{Executive Summary}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The three stories converge on a unified strategic prescription: margin optimisation
must operate simultaneously along the \textbf{Channel $\times$ Discount $\times$
Inventory-Turnover} axis.
Channel reallocation (Story~1) lifts revenue quality without additional spend;
discount guardrails (Story~2) recover \$96.1M gross profit from the existing
promotional budget; and coordinated overstock liquidation with return-reduction
interventions (Story~3) unlocks up to \$74.8M in combined cash flow improvement.
Aggregate addressable upside across the three levers is estimated at
\textbf{\$170–\$230M}, achievable within a 12-month operational horizon.
